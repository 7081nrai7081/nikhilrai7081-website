Go-to-Market From Zero: Your First 100 Users

The hardest part of launching anything isn't building it — it's the silence right after. You ship the product, open the doors, post the link, and… nothing. No audience, no list, no momentum. Going from zero is its own discipline, and most of the advice written about growth quietly assumes you already have a few thousand people paying attention. This is about the part before that: how to get your first hundred real users when nobody knows you exist.

Don't launch to everyone — pick a beachhead

The instinct at zero is to cast the widest possible net: "anyone could use this." That instinct is exactly backwards. A broad message to everyone lands with no one, because it isn't sharp enough for any single person to feel it's for them. Pick a beachhead instead — one narrow group with a problem urgent enough that they'll forgive a rough edges and an unknown name.

Narrow isn't a limitation; it's leverage. A specific group is easier to find, easier to talk to, and far more likely to tell each other about you. You can always expand outward once you've won a beachhead — but you can't expand from nowhere.

Find the watering holes

Your first users already gather somewhere — a WhatsApp group, a subreddit, a Discord, a local meetup, a college, a neighbourhood. Your job at zero isn't to build an audience from scratch; it's to show up where the right people already are. Make a list of those watering holes and go to them in person or online, as a participant first and a founder second.

This is unglamorous and it's the whole game. One genuinely useful post in the right community will out-perform a paid campaign blasted at strangers who never asked. Distribution beats reach — being in the right room matters more than being seen by a big one.

Do the things that don't scale

At zero, manual effort is a feature, not a failure. The early days are for doing things you'll never be able to do at ten thousand users — and that's precisely why they work. This is the same patient, hands-on energy that real growth marketing is built on, just concentrated into your first few weeks.

1

Invite by hand

Message people one at a time. Personal beats broadcast every time at zero.

2

Onboard personally

Walk early users in yourself. You'll learn more in five conversations than five surveys.

3

Close the loop fast

Hear a problem, fix it that day, tell them you did. Speed earns loyalty.

4

Ask for the intro

Every happy early user knows two more. Make asking part of the routine.

Give people a reason to be early

Being first should feel like a privilege, not a risk. Early users are taking a chance on something unproven, so give them something in return — founding-member status, early access, a direct line to you, a say in what gets built. The reward doesn't have to be a discount; often the strongest pull is simply belonging to something before everyone else does.

This reframes the whole pitch. Instead of "please try my unfinished thing," you're offering a chance to shape it. People don't just use products that way — they champion them.

Create momentum, then borrow it

Nothing attracts people like other people. Once you have a handful of real users, make that visible and let it compound — a first event that actually happens, a few honest words from someone who showed up, a number that's true and growing. Early traction is the most persuasive marketing you have, so don't hide it.

The same logic powers strategic partnerships at this stage: find someone who already has the trust and audience you don't, and create something where helping you helps them too. Borrowed credibility is how a small thing punches above its weight.

Measure the right things at zero

Vanity metrics are especially seductive when you have almost nothing — a spike of sign-ups feels like proof. But at zero the only questions that matter are whether people come back and whether they bring someone. Retention and word-of-mouth are the leading indicators of something real; raw sign-ups are not. The same way a mature lead-gen system watches pipeline rather than activity, your launch should watch repeat use and referrals, not applause.

Going from zero isn't about a big launch — it's about a narrow one. Pick a beachhead, go where those people already are, do the unscalable work to delight the first few, give them a reason to be early, and turn that small, real momentum into the next hundred.
