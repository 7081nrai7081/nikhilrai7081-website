    <div class="cs-block">
      <p>Most partnerships run on promises: two companies agree to refer each other and hope the relationship does the rest. An <strong>integration partnership</strong> is different — it runs on something a buyer can actually see working. When your product connects to a partner's, the case for both of you sells itself in the product, not in a slide. That's a fundamentally stronger channel, but only if you build the integration like a product, not a checkbox on a partnership deck.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cs-block">
      <h2>Why integrations are a different kind of partnership</h2>
      <p>A referral partnership depends on someone remembering to make the introduction. An integration partnership depends on nothing — it sits inside the workflow, visible every time a customer of either company opens the tool. That's the leverage: the proof of the relationship is functional, not verbal. It's also why integrations survive turnover on both sides. People who signed the deal can leave; the connection between the two products keeps working regardless. This is the same leverage idea behind any <a href="/blog-channel-partnerships">channel partnership</a> — you're borrowing distribution instead of building all of it yourself — but here the "channel" is the product surface itself.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cs-block">
      <h2>Build the integration before you build the relationship</h2>
      <p>The instinct is to start with a call: pitch the partnership, get a verbal yes, then figure out the technical work. Flip it. A working integration — even a rough one — is the pitch. It proves the overlap is real instead of asserted, and it gives the partner something concrete to react to instead of a hypothetical. Companies say yes to partnerships in meetings all the time and never act on them; a live connection between two products doesn't have that problem, because it's already doing the thing the meeting was trying to agree to.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cs-block">
      <h2>Where the integration actually needs to live</h2>
      <p>Building the connection is half the work — the other half is making it findable by the people who'd use it.</p>
      <div class="cs-steps">
        <div class="cs-step"><span class="num">1</span><div><h3>Get listed in the partner's marketplace</h3><p>If they have an app directory or integration gallery, that's a permanent, self-serve source of intent — customers land there already looking for exactly what you offer.</p></div></div>
        <div class="cs-step"><span class="num">2</span><div><h3>Publish joint documentation</h3><p>A setup guide that lives on both sites, written for the customer doing the connecting, not for either company's marketing team.</p></div></div>
        <div class="cs-step"><span class="num">3</span><div><h3>Do one piece of joint content</h3><p>A webinar, a co-written guide, a shared case study — something that puts the integration in front of both audiences at once instead of waiting for organic discovery.</p></div></div>
        <div class="cs-step"><span class="num">4</span><div><h3>Enable both sales teams</h3><p>Reps on either side should know the integration exists and what problem it solves, or the best partnership in the product goes unmentioned in every sales call.</p></div></div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="cs-block">
      <h2>The maintenance nobody budgets for</h2>
      <p>An integration isn't a one-time build — it's a standing commitment. APIs version, fields get deprecated, the partner ships a redesign, and if nobody's watching, the connection quietly breaks while both companies still list it as a feature. Budget for someone to own it after launch: monitoring for failures, a channel to hear about the partner's breaking changes before customers do, and a real testing pass whenever either side ships something that touches the integration. A partnership that breaks silently and stays broken for a month does more damage to trust than never having built it.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cs-block">
      <h2>Choose the few that earn their keep</h2>
      <p>It's tempting to integrate with everything adjacent to your category — a long logo list looks impressive. But each integration is ongoing cost, and a directory of twenty half-maintained connections is worse than five that work perfectly and that your sales team actually mentions. Pick integrations the way you'd pick any channel partner: by real overlap with your <a href="/blog-ideal-customer-profile">ideal customer profile</a>, not by who else is easy to connect to. The goal isn't integration sprawl. It's a small number of connections that customers actively choose you because of.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cs-block">
      <div class="cs-callout"><p>Integration partnerships sell through the product instead of a slide — build the connection first, then use it as proof to get listed, documented and enabled on both sides. Budget real maintenance for it after launch, and choose a small number of integrations with genuine audience overlap over a long list of half-working connections. A directory of logos isn't a channel. A working connection your customers rely on is.</p></div>
    </div>
